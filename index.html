How the matching works

Every image is shrunk to a tiny grayscale thumbnail and turned into a 64-bit perceptual fingerprint — the little grids on each card. Two images match when their fingerprints barely differ, measured bit-by-bit (Hamming distance). Because the fingerprint captures structure rather than raw pixels, a resized or re-saved image still lines up. Two unrelated images still share about half their 64 bits by chance, so scores only become meaningful above roughly 80% — the threshold can't be dragged below that. With Flip-aware on, each image is also fingerprinted mirrored, so a horizontally-flipped copy still matches — those pairs are marked ⇄ Mirrored.

Average aHash

Reduce to 8×8, take the mean brightness, flag each pixel brighter or darker than the mean. Fast; best for exact and lightly edited copies.

Difference dHash

Compare each pixel to its right-hand neighbour on a 9×8 grid. Encodes gradients, so it shrugs off exposure and lighting shifts.

Perceptual pHash

Run a 32×32 Discrete Cosine Transform, keep the low-frequency corner, threshold on its median. The most robust — it reads structure, not pixels.
